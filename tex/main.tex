\section{Introduction}

The axion was originally proposed as a consequence of the Peccei--Quinn mechanism to resolve the strong $CP$\footnote{Here $C$ denotes charge conjugation and $P$ denotes parity.} problem in quantum chromodynamics\,\cite{PecceiQuinn1977_ConstraintsCP_PRD,Weinberg1978_NewLightBoson,PecceiQuinn1977_CPConservation_PRL,Wilczek1978_ProblemStrongPAndT}. 
Later, more general axionlike particles (ALPs) were proposed in various extensions of the Standard Model as pseudo-Nambu--Goldstone bosons associated with spontaneously broken global symmetries\,\cite{svrcek2006axions,Kim1987_LightPseudoscalars,JaeckelRingwald2010_ALPReview,Arvanitaki2010_StringAxiverse}. 
Concerning the cosmological implications, axions and ALPs are also well-motivated dark matter candidates\,\cite{Preskill1983_invisible_axion,Abbott1983_CosmologicalBoundAxion,Marsh2016_AxionCosmology}.
In this paper, we refer to axions and ALPs as ``axions'' for simplicity. 

Axions may possess couplings to ordinary Standard Model particles\,\cite{Graham2013_Newobservables}, including fermions and gauge bosons. 
Crucially, axion field gradients ($\mathbf{\nabla}a$) may couple to fermionic spins (half-integer spins) through a pseudoscalar interaction. The Hamiltonian for this interaction can be expressed as:
\begin{equation}
    H_{\mathrm{int}} = g_\mathrm{aNN} \mathbf{\nabla}a \cdot \mathbf{S}\,,
\end{equation}
where $g_\mathrm{aNN}$ is the axion-spin coupling strength, and $\mathbf{S}$ is the spin operator. 
The axion field gradient can be regarded as a pseudomagnetic field that mimics the behavior of a real magnetic field, where the Hamiltonian can be written as:
\begin{equation}
    H = -\hbar \gamma \mathbf{B} \cdot \mathbf{S} \,,
\end{equation} 
where $\gamma$ is the gyromagnetic ratio of the spin, and $\mathbf{B}$ is the magnetic field. 
The pseudomagnetic field induces spin dynamics, providing a pathway for detection in precision measurement experiments such as nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) and comagnetometer-based searches\,\cite{ouellet2019first,du2018search,ALPHA2023,aja2022canfranc,CAPP2024,brouwer2022projected,alesini2023future,zhong2018results,garcia2024searchaxiondarkmatter,quiskamp2024near,rettaroli2024search,Gramolin2021_Ferromagnetic,gavilan2024searching,bloch2105nasduck,wu2019search,abel2023search,karanth2023first,lee2023laboratory,capozzi2023new, CASPErProposal2014, kimball2018overview, wang2018application, wu2019search, aybas2021_SolidStateNMR, CASPEr_ZULF_2019,Walter2025_CASPErG}. 
% In this picture, the local dark matter density determines the amplitude of the field. A variety of astrophysical and cosmological scenarios predict different spatial and temporal structures of the ALP field. The simplest and most commonly considered model is the galactic dark matter halo of the Milky Way, in which the ALP field forms a virialized distribution with a characteristic velocity dispersion. In addition, more structured configurations may arise, including gravitationally bound halos around the Earth or the Sun, cold streams originating from disrupted subhalos, and gravitationally focused dark matter distributions due to the influence of massive bodies. 
There have been dedicated studies on the axion-induced spin dynamics\,\cite{Dror2023_SensitivityAxion, Yuzhe2023_FrequencyScanning}\YZ{are there more such studies?}, providing axion signal signatures and experimental sensitivity to axion fields. 
However, to our knowledge, there have not been studies taking advantage of numerical methods to verify the derived axion signal signatures. 
On the other hand, as more axion models and experiments are being proposed, such as cosmic axion background\,\cite{Dror2021_CosmicAxionBackground,Nitta2023_ADMX_CosmicAxionBackground} and fine-grained axion\,\cite{OHare2025_Finegrained} \YZ{more new ideas of axion search?}, a tool is needed to systematically simulate the axion-induced spin dynamics under various physical conditions and axion field configurations.

In this work, we present \texttt{axionbloch}, an open-source package designed to simulate spin dynamics induced by axion fields. 
The interface is implemented in Python, a programming language known for its simplicity and readability, while the underlying numerical integration is implemented in C++ for efficiency. 
The package provides a flexible framework for modeling axion fields and experimental configurations. 
% , including Milky Way galactic halo signals, localized halos, streams, and gravitational focusing effects. 
By numerically solving the relevant spin evolution equations, \texttt{axionbloch} enables the prediction and analysis of experimentally observable signals.

The \texttt{axionbloch} package adopts the International System of Units (SI) throughout. All physical inputs, internal computations, and outputs are consistently expressed in SI base and derived units.

Considering the physical problem we are dealing with, common atomic-physics- and astrophysics-specific units are included. \subsection{Algorithm}

\label{sec:algorithm}

\subsubsection{Spin Dynamics}
\label{sec:spindynamics}
Considering the current experiments looking for axion-induced spin dynamics, \axionbloch{} focuses on spin-$1/2$ magnetic resonance. Different spins may be addressed in future updates. We use the measurable, magnetization, to characterize the spin ensemble. 
% We model the magnetization using using the Bloch equations
In the presence of magnetic fields, including both bias fields applied in experiments and the axion-induced pseudomagnetic field, the magnetization $\mathbf{M}$ evolves as
\begin{align}
\frac{d M_x}{dt} &= \gamma \left( \mathbf{M} \times \mathbf{B}_{\mathrm{eff}} \right)_x - \frac{M_x}{T_2}\,,\label{eq:bloch_x} \\
\frac{d M_y}{dt} &= \gamma \left( \mathbf{M} \times \mathbf{B}_{\mathrm{eff}} \right)_y - \frac{M_y}{T_2}\,,\label{eq:bloch_y} \\
\frac{d M_z}{dt} &= \gamma \left( \mathbf{M} \times \mathbf{B}_{\mathrm{eff}} \right)_z - \frac{M_z - M_0}{T_1}\,,\label{eq:bloch_z}
\end{align}
where $\gamma$ is the gyromagnetic ratio, $\mathbf{B}_{\mathrm{eff}}$ is the total effective field, $T_1$ and $T_2$ are relaxation times. 
The bias field is usually much stronger than the pseudomagnetic field. Therefore, we adopt the rotating frame to remove the Larmor precession due to the bias field, allowing efficient simulation of the slower axion-induced dynamics of interest. 

The transverse relaxation time, characterized by time constant $T_2^*$, includes the $T_2$ relaxation and the ``dephase'' due to inhomogeneous bias field which is sometimes characterized by $T_\delta$. Such inhomogeneity in the bias field is simulated by sampling the spread of the bias fields in the \texttt{Magnet} instance, and solving the Bloch equations for individual bias fields. 

\subsubsection{Numerical Integration}
\label{sec:numericalintegration}
To derive the time evolution of the magnetization, the Bloch equations is solved numerically using the fourth-order Runge--Kutta (RK4) method. Given a time step $\Delta t$, effective magnetic field, and initial magnetization $\mathbf{M}_0$, the magnetization at the $(n+1)$-th step can be found via
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{M}^{n+1}
=
\mathbf{M}^n
+
\frac{\Delta t}{6}
\left(
\mathbf{k}_1 + 2\mathbf{k}_2 + 2\mathbf{k}_3 + \mathbf{k}_4
\right)\,.
\end{equation}
Here 
\begin{align}
\mathbf{k}_1 &= \left.\frac{d\mathbf{M}}{dt}\right|_{\mathbf{M}=\mathbf{M}^n}\,, \\
\mathbf{k}_2 &= \left.\frac{d\mathbf{M}}{dt}\right|_{\mathbf{M}=\mathbf{M}^n + \frac{1}{2}\mathbf{k}_1\Delta t}\,, \\
\mathbf{k}_3 &= \left.\frac{d\mathbf{M}}{dt}\right|_{\mathbf{M}=\mathbf{M}^n + \frac{1}{2}\mathbf{k}_2\Delta t}\,, \\
\mathbf{k}_4 &= \left.\frac{d\mathbf{M}}{dt}\right|_{\mathbf{M}=\mathbf{M}^n + \mathbf{k}_3\Delta t}\,,
\end{align}
where $d\mathbf{M}/dt$ can be found using the Bloch equations [Eqs.\,(\ref{eq:bloch_x}--\ref{eq:bloch_z})]. 
This fourth-order method yields a local truncation error of $\mathcal{O}(\Delta t^5)$, while the accumulated error over $N = 1/\Delta t$ steps is of order $\mathcal{O}(\Delta t^4)$. 
A smaller $\Delta t$ enables stable simulations over long durations, whereas a larger $\Delta t$ reduces the number of steps and thus the computational cost. 
The optimal choice of $\Delta t$ balances simulation stability and computational efficiency.

\label{sec:implementation}

Using object-oriented programming, we implement the algorithm in a modular way to separate experimental configuration, axion field modeling, and numerical simulation, allowing for flexible configuration of simulations and axion field models.

On the Python side, classes \texttt{Sample}, \texttt{Magnet}, and \texttt{MagField} encapsulate the relevant experimental parameters, including sample relaxation times, magnetic fields strength and homogeneity. 
The axion field models are implemented by dedicated classes, such as \texttt{MilkyWayAxionHalo}, which carry the properties of the axion fields. Together with the method \texttt{MagField.setAxionFields()}, the pseudomagnetic fields can be generated. 
Different axion models can be incorporated as separate classes without modifying the simulation framework. 
Simulation is handled within the \texttt{axionbloch.SimuTools} module. 
In particular, the classes \texttt{Simulation} and \texttt{Simulations} manage the interaction between experimental configurations and axion field models, organizing the parameters and execution of single or multiple simulation runs. 

The RK4 algorithm for solving the Bloch equations is implemented in a dedicated C++ library. 
% This library is more efficient compared to a pure Python implementation. 
The application programming interface (API) for this library is \texttt{blochSimulation}. 
This C++ backend is interfaced with Python using \texttt{pybind11},\footnote{\url{http://pybind11.readthedocs.io/}} enabling integration between high-level Python workflow management and low-level efficient numerical computation. 


\subsection{Calibration}
\label{sec:calibration}
\YZ{is calibration section too long? }

Accurate calibration is essential to ensure that the simulated signals reliably reflect the underlying physical system and can be meaningfully compared with experimental observables. 
In particular, calibration establishes the correspondence between the NMR signal and model parameters, including magnetic fields, nuclear spin gyromagnetic ratio $\gamma$ and relaxation times ($T_1$, $T_2$ and $T_2^*$). 
It also allows one to verify the correctness and stability of the numerical implementation. 
In this section, we describe benchmarks against known analytical or experimental results. These ensure that the simulations produce quantitatively accurate results\,\cite{Abragam1961_NuclearMagnetism}.
For the benchmark examples in the section, we use an ethanol sample, and an axial bias field $\mathbf{B_{0}} = B_0 \mathbf{\hat{e}}_z$. 
% Except for the example in \cref{sec:hyperpol}, the sample is initially thermally polarized and in equilibrium state. 

\subsubsection{Pulsed-NMR: Free Decay and Spin Echo}
\label{sec:pNMR}
In the pulsed-NMR calibration, we apply one $\SI{90}{\degree}$ pulse and one $\SI{180}{\degree}$ magnetic-field pulse in the simulation. The magnetization is expected to:
\begin{itemize}
    \item oscillate at the Larmor frequency;
    \item be tipped by $\SI{90}{\degree}$ to the x-y plane by the $\SI{90}{\degree}$ pulse;
    \item free decay with relaxation time $T_2^*$ in the absence of pulses;
    \item refocus (echo) after $\SI{180}{\degree}$ pulses; 
    \item show decaying amplitude of echoes with relaxation time $T_2$.
\end{itemize}
We can observe the behaviors above in the calibration example illustrated in \cref{fig:calibration_T2_T2star}\,(a). \YZ{should we add quantitative analysis of the calibration?}
With this benchmark, we calibrate the magnetic pulses, Larmor precession, and transverse relaxations.

\subsection{Axion Simulation}
\label{sec:simulation}

Similar to the calibration examples, we simulate the axion-induced NMR signals by solving the Bloch equations with the axion-induced pseudomagnetic field as part of the effective magnetic field. 
We present an example of a commonly considered model -- axion constituting the Milky Way galactic dark matter halo\,\cite{Evans2019_SHMRefined,Gramolin2022Feb_SpectralSignatures}, implemented in the class \texttt{MilkyWayAxionHalo}. 
In this model, the axion field is treated as an oscillating field with a frequency determined by the axion mass, and a linewidth (characterizing the stochasticity) determined by the velocity dispersion of the virialized dark matter halo. 
The sample is chosen to be \ch{^{129}Xe}, polarized to $\SI{50}{\%}$ initially, and the bias field is applied along the z direction. 
The details of the simulation configuration can be found in the code example in \cref{code:axion_example}. 
We refer readers to \cref{fig:architecture} which illustrates the relationship between the objects in the code example. 
The simulated pseudomagnetic fields and the amplitude of the induced transverse magnetization are shown in \cref{fig:MilkyWayAxionHalo}\YZ{Would you prefer \cref{fig:MilkyWayAxionHalo_2col}?}, in which we can find the signal increasing with time at the beginning of the simulation due to axion field gradient, and then decaying due to the $T_1$ relaxation. 
The efficiency of the simulation is limited by the numerical integration of the Bloch equations. 
The total number of integration steps can be found by:\YZ{should here be a :?}
\begin{align}
    N &= \texttt{Simulation.rate} \times \texttt{Simulation.duration} \nonumber\\
    &\quad \times \texttt{MagField.numFields} \times \texttt{Magnet.numPt}\,,
\end{align}
where \texttt{Simulation.rate} is the sampling rate of the simulation, \texttt{Simulation.duration} is the duration of the simulation, \texttt{MagField.numFields} is the number of random pseudomagnetic fields generated to account for the stochasticity of the axion field, and \texttt{Magnet.numPt} is the number of bias fields sampled to account for the inhomogeneity of the bias field.
For the example in \cref{code:axion_example}, the total number of integration steps is \SI{4.064e9}{}, and the time consumed is \SI{10}{\sec} on a personal computer (CPU: Intel\textsuperscript{\textregistered} Core\textsuperscript{\texttrademark} Ultra 7 155H; Base speed: 1.40 GHz). Such moderate time consumption allows users to freely explore different parameter regimes with many simulations.

\section{Development}
\label{sec:dev-approach}
%-----------------------

% Version Control
Development of \texttt{axionbloch} is managed using \texttt{git},\footnote{\url{http://git-scm.com}} with the code hosted on a public GitHub repository.\footnote{\url{https://github.com/Yuzhe98/AxionBloch}} Changes to the main codebase follow a \textit{pull request} workflow, allowing contributions to be reviewed before being merged. Users can participate by forking the repository, making modifications in their fork, and submitting pull requests for review.
As \texttt{axionbloch} is intended as a collaborative resource, user contributions and suggestions are encouraged and appreciated. 

% Automated Testing with pytest
\texttt{axionbloch} includes a suite of automated tests to ensure correctness and reliability. The tests are implemented using the \texttt{pytest} framework,\footnote{\url{https://docs.pytest.org/}} which allows for calibration of physical processes and testing of functions, classes, and modules. 
The test suite covers core functionalities such as:
\begin{itemize}
    \item initialization and manipulation of classes; 
    \item numerical solution of the Bloch equations;
    \item magnetic fields and relaxations;
    \item handling of each axion field configurations. 
\end{itemize}
Tests can be executed locally. In the future, we may add tools for online testing considering the demand of testing. Regular execution of the test suite ensures that new changes do not introduce regressions, maintaining the stability and correctness of the package.

% documentation
Documentation for \texttt{axionbloch} is available on Read the Docs.\footnote{\url{http://axionbloch.readthedocs.io}} It is built using \texttt{Sphinx}\footnote{\url{http://www.sphinx-doc.org}} and provides detailed explanations of the API, along with instructions for installation, configuration, and usage.

% Future
As we are having numerous new axion field models and experiments emerging, we are planning to extend the functionality, so that \axionbloch{} can be a tool for updating our knowledge of the induced spin dynamics.

\section{Conclusions}
\label{sec:conclusions}
%-----------------------

We have presented \texttt{axionbloch}, a package providing a framework to simulate spin dynamics induced by axion fields under a variety of physical scenarios. 
The package adopts SI units so that the inputs and outputs are comparable to experimental parameters and observables. 
Numerical integration of the Bloch equations is performed using a fourth-order Runge--Kutta method in the rotating frame, enabling stable and efficient simulation. 
Comprehensive calibrations have been done to ensure the accuracy of the simulations. 
An example simulation of axion-induced signals from a local virialized axion dark matter halo is presented for demonstration of package's capabilities.

By providing an open-source, well-documented, and tested tool, \texttt{axionbloch} aims to facilitate the study of axion-induced spin dynamics in precision measurement experiments and to serve as a platform for further method development and incorporation of new axion models.
